# AI Writing — Signs to Avoid

A working checklist based on [Wikipedia: Signs of AI Writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing) and patterns identified during editing sessions on brajeshwar.com.

---

## Typography

- No em dashes (—). Use commas, colons, parentheses, or restructure the sentence.
- Curly/typographic double quotes (" ") not straight (").
- Curly/typographic apostrophes (') not straight (').

---

## Structure

- No "Conclusion" sections that restate the title dressed up as wisdom.
- No formulaic rule of three — three examples, three case studies, three bullets in a row.
- No parallel constructions repeated back to back ("X replaces Y / X replaces Y / X replaces Y").
- No numbered paragraphs that are a list in disguise ("They do X... They do Y... They do Z...").
- No section headers that read like a Medium essay outline ("What This Means for Us All").
- No bullet lists where prose would do fine.
- Case studies serve the argument. They don't become the argument.

---

## Tone & Language

- No puffery — "breathtaking," "remarkable," "reshaping the landscape," "game-changing," "innovative."
- No press-release tone when writing about companies or people.
- No overgeneralization — presenting one observed pattern as universal law ("Everyone does X," "All teams know Y").
- No filler connectives — "moreover," "furthermore," "additionally," "it is worth noting," "in conclusion."
- No AI-tell words — "delve," "dive into," "landscape," "nuanced," "comprehensive," "robust," "seamless," "leverage" (as a verb), "unleash," "unlock," "cutting-edge," "transformative."
- No celebrity name-dropping purely for authority without substance behind it.
- No rhetorical questions as section openers.

---

## Voice

- Personal anchor present — a memory, observation, or story that belongs to the writer.
- The writer is present. You can feel who wrote it.
- Writing takes a position and holds it, rather than hedging in every direction.
- No conclusions that restate the headline as a life lesson.

---

## What Good Looks Like

- Short sentences that breathe.
- Specific details over smooth generalisations.
- Humour that is dry and earned, not decorative.
- Footnotes used for depth, not padding.
- Asides and pull-quotes to break rhythm, not to pad word count.
